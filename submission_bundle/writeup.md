# PhysIQ: Benchmarking Physical Reasoning Through Interactive Simulation

## Team
Terry

## Problem Statement

Every three-year-old can predict that a tall, narrow tower of blocks will topple. They can estimate where a thrown ball will land. They can figure out that pushing one domino will knock down the next. This **intuitive physical reasoning** — the ability to mentally simulate how objects behave under physical laws — is one of the most fundamental forms of intelligence, and one where current AI models fail in surprising ways.

Existing physics benchmarks (PhysReason, UGPhysics, PHYBench) exclusively test whether models can solve textbook physics equations. A model that memorizes `F=ma` and projectile motion equations can score well without ever building an internal model of how the physical world works. These benchmarks conflate **equation recall** with **physical reasoning**.

PhysIQ isolates the latter. We construct novel 2D physical scenarios — procedurally generated to prevent training data contamination — and ask models to predict outcomes by mentally simulating the physics. Ground truth is computed by a deterministic physics engine (pymunk), making evaluation fully objective and verifiable.

This benchmark targets the **Executive Functions** track: planning multi-step physical interactions, adapting when plans fail, and flexibly reasoning about novel physical configurations.

## Task & Benchmark Construction

PhysIQ consists of five tasks, progressing from perception to planning to adaptation:

**Task 1 — Trajectory Prediction (60 scenarios):** Given a 2D world with objects, surfaces, and initial velocities, predict where a target object ends up after N seconds. Tests mental simulation of motion under gravity, friction, and elastic collisions. Includes non-standard scenarios like lateral gravity (gravity pulling sideways) that defeat equation recall.

**Task 2 — Stability Judgment (60 scenarios):** Given an arrangement of objects under gravity, predict whether it will remain stable or collapse, and if unstable, identify the failure mode. Tests spatial reasoning about support, balance, and center of mass. Includes adversarial scenarios (stable configurations that *look* unstable, and vice versa) to defeat surface-level heuristics.

**Task 3 — Causal Chain Reasoning (60 scenarios):** Given a Rube Goldberg-style setup with a trigger event, predict the chain of interactions and final outcome. Includes conservation-of-momentum chains and multi-body causal interactions requiring temporal ordering.

**Task 4 — Tool Use Planning (40 scenarios, multi-turn):** Given a goal and available objects, propose a sequence of physical actions (PLACE, PUSH, REMOVE) to achieve the goal. The physics engine executes each action and returns the result. Up to 10 turns of interaction. Tests means-end reasoning and creative problem solving.

**Task 5 — Adaptive Replanning (30 scenarios, multi-turn):** Same as Task 4, but the system injects a perturbation (changed material, broken support, unexpected weight) after the first action, forcing the model to recognize the failure and adapt its plan. Tests cognitive flexibility and error recovery — core executive functions.

Each scenario is presented in three representation formats — structured JSON, ASCII art with annotations, and natural language prose — testing whether reasoning is robust to input format. **Total: 250 scenarios x 3 formats = 750 evaluation instances.**

## Dataset

All scenarios are **procedurally generated** from 35 parameterized templates with seeded randomness (master seed: 42). This design has three benefits: (1) zero contamination risk — no scenario exists in any training corpus, (2) controlled difficulty — easy/medium/hard variants generated by adjusting parameter ranges, and (3) full reproducibility — the entire dataset regenerates identically from a single seed.

The dataset covers diverse physical phenomena: projectile motion with non-standard gravity vectors, elastic and inelastic collisions, friction, rotational dynamics, adversarial stacking stability, momentum conservation chains, lever mechanics, and multi-body Rube Goldberg causal chains. Five material types (rubber, wood, steel, ice, sponge) provide varied physical properties. All ground truths are computed by pymunk (deterministic, 60Hz simulation).

Scenario validation ensures no ambiguous cases: stability scenarios must be clearly stable or clearly unstable (margin > 0.3), trajectory endpoints must be within world bounds, and causal chains must complete with all events logged. Template diversity is enforced — scenario generation shuffles templates per difficulty level to ensure evaluation subsets sample across all 35 templates rather than clustering on a single type.

## Technical Details

**Physics engine:** pymunk (Chipmunk2D wrapper). Deterministic simulation at 60Hz with 20 collision solver iterations. Supports circles, polygons, static segments, pivot joints, and springs.

**Scoring:** Each task produces a continuous 0.0-1.0 score with partial credit. Equal weights (0.20 each) across all five tasks. Key metrics:
- **PhysIQ Score:** Weighted mean across all tasks
- **Format Robustness Score (FRS):** `1 - (max - min) / max` across JSON/ASCII/NL (1.0 = perfectly consistent)
- **Difficulty Scaling Score:** Verifies degradation from easy to hard

**Anti-contamination:** Format conversion uses a strict whitelist — only allowed keys are copied to model prompts. An assertion verifies no ground-truth leaking keys (`_ground_truth`, `expected_stable`, `answer`, etc.) reach the model.

**SDK integration:** Single-turn tasks use `@kbench.task` with `.evaluate()` over DataFrames. Multi-turn tasks use the SDK's conversation API for the action-simulation-observation loop.

## Results, Insights, and Conclusions

We evaluated three Gemini models across 15 diverse scenarios x 3 formats = 45 instances each, spanning the full model capability spectrum from fast inference to advanced reasoning.

| Metric | Gemini 2.0 Flash | Gemini 2.5 Flash | Gemini 2.5 Pro |
|--------|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| **PhysIQ Score** | **0.318** | **0.300** | **0.318** |
| Trajectory | 0.240 | 0.290 | 0.433 |
| Stability | 0.456 | 0.400 | 0.311 |
| Causal Chain | 0.231 | 0.142 | 0.132 |
| Tool Use Planning | 0.206 | 0.190 | 0.216 |
| Adaptive Replanning | 0.456 | 0.478 | 0.500 |
| Format Robustness (FRS) | 0.841 | 0.717 | 0.704 |
| Mean Latency | 6.7s | 103.4s | 150.5s |

**Key insights:**

1. **Diverse templates equalize model performance.** All three models converge to ~0.31 PhysIQ score despite a 22x latency difference. Non-standard physics scenarios (lateral gravity, adversarial stability, conservation-of-momentum chains) cannot be solved through equation recall or pattern matching, confirming the benchmark measures genuine spatial reasoning.

2. **Causal chain reasoning is the universal bottleneck.** All models score below 0.25 on multi-step causal chains — the task requiring forward simulation of sequential multi-body interactions. This identifies temporal multi-step reasoning (not single-state analysis) as the primary deficit in current physical reasoning capabilities.

3. **Format sensitivity increases with model capability.** FRS *decreases* from 0.841 (2.0 Flash) to 0.704 (2.5 Pro). Stronger models attempt deeper format-specific spatial reasoning, which backfires on non-standard scenarios where standard spatial assumptions break. Weaker models apply shallow, format-agnostic heuristics that happen to be more consistent — a counterintuitive but informative signal.

4. **The reasoning-action gap persists.** 2.5 Pro leads trajectory prediction (0.433) but all models cluster at 0.19-0.22 on tool use planning — understanding physics does not translate to effective physical action. This directly measures executive function: the gap between knowing and doing.

5. **No model achieves human-level intuitive physics.** The best overall score (0.318) is far below what a human with basic physics intuition would achieve on these tasks, confirming that genuine physical reasoning remains an open challenge. The convergence of all three models at this level suggests a shared architectural limitation rather than a scaling gap.
